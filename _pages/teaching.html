Context Statement

As a computational ecologist, I research how big data can help us understand climate change’s impacts on forest biodiversity. I am a fourth-year PhD candidate in the Nicholas School of the Environment and through my work, I am constantly reminded of how urgently we need people who can think ecologically about the changing world. This need shapes my teaching. I have taught students as a teaching assistant and discussion group leader in diverse environments from the classroom to the computer lab to the field. I teach both undergraduate and master’s level students, from large 90-person lectures to small 10-person seminars. For me, education is the most powerful form of connection, a way to transform abstract concepts into moments of wonder. Central to my teaching philosophy is fostering a sense of discovery in others and creating inclusive, supportive spaces where complex ideas inspire students to ask new questions. I am committed to making learning accessible and engaging, so students may see themselves as active participants in their own learning.

Areas of Activity

Designing and planning for learning

In my weekly 75-minute discussions for Introduction to Environmental Sciences and Policy, I guided 18 undergraduates through supporting course material. In this 90-person lecture course, discussion sections were often students’ only opportunity to engage personally with the content and their peers. For many students, they were also considering environmental science in the context of their personal lives for the first time, so I recognized the need for both safety and structure so that they could engage meaningfully with their peers. Thus, I scaffolded our discussions with AORTA Collective’s Anti-Oppressive Facilitation for Democratic Process community guidelines to establish norms for equitable participation and respectful dialogue [A1/V1]. I received feedback from students that they “loved how we discussed expectations on day 1” because it is not a common practice in their other classes.

To understand students’ needs from the start, I provided a pre-survey asking about their backgrounds, previous positive discussion experiences, and visions for inclusivity in our group. Their responses directly shaped how I designed activities. Students expressed a preference for small group discussion before having to share with the larger group, so I incorporated think-pair-share for individual reflection which then progressed to collaborative small-group conversations before culminating in peer-led whole-group discussions [A1]. This way, each stage built students’ confidence before asking them to share out.

For their final projects, I designed a feedback loop where students delivered two-minute pitches to the group in discussion to receive peer feedback before finishing their projects. I also offered flexible format options (research paper, podcast, video, etc.) to honor different strengths, preferences, and learning styles [A1/V2]. The range of impressive and engaging submissions I received showed me that this flexibility supported genuine engagement without compromising rigor, and that students took ownership of projects when given choice in how they demonstrated learning.

Teaching and Supporting Student Learning

Leading twice-weekly 75-minute lab sections for Applied Statistical Modeling for Environmental Management has shown me how responsive teaching involves a consistent assessment of student understanding and adapting in the moment. In these lab sections, I guide master’s students through R programming exercises that extend lecture concepts into practical application. Because students enter the course with various coding backgrounds, I have learned how to explain statistical concepts through a variety of approaches, from visual representations, to worked examples, to analogies of familiar scenarios. Recently, when a student asked about the difference between a confidence interval and a p-value and my explanations were not clicking, I made an analogy to fishing which resonated with her more than anything else I said [A2/V2]. Another example is recently when students were struggling to understand what a QQ plot was showing on the X and Y axis, I developed a short slide deck for the next session to break down the concept visually, showing step-by-step what the plot does and why it matters for assessing normality in data. In the next lab session, I presented the explanation and provided a practice problem for students to work through on the blackboard together. Watching them talk through the problem, pointing at the data and asking what the deviations meant, showed the tangible process of moving from confusion to conceptual understanding [A2].

This experience reinforced that effective teaching requires actively monitoring students’ comprehension and being willing to pause their activities to address gaps. I include frequent check-in questions during labs: “What do you expect to see here? Why might this assumption matter?” [A2] These moments both help students and me identify where their understanding might break down and allows me to provide targeted support for my students.

Assessing and Giving Feedback for Learning

I use assessment as an ongoing conversation about learning rather than a final judgement of student performance. As a TA for Data Visualization and Storytelling for master’s students, I provided written feedback to explain why certain approaches were more effective and offered specific strategies that students could apply to future work [A3]. Even when visualizations were technically correct, I included suggestions for improvement by grounding my feedback in lecture principles. With each assignment, I saw students apply the concepts I previously commented on, showing that if students receive improvement-focused feedback, they can engage more intentionally with the content.

In exam-based courses like Plant Communities of North Carolina, I collaborated with the instructor to grade exams, discussing challenging student responses as a team to ensure fair and consistent evaluations [A3/V5]. These conversations are valuable for calibrating assessment to support student learning across different levels.

Support and guide learners

As a TA for Plant Communities of North Carolina, I guided 12 undergraduates in field-based learning across diverse ecosystems, from Piedmont forests to coastal plains [A4/K2]. Guiding students from field collection to lab analysis, I helped them connect textbook concepts to real-world specimens, fostering both technical skills and ecological literacy [K2/K4]. During field sessions, I circulated among the students, asking questions to encourage them to think beyond rote memorization. Over time, I observed students’ growth from memorizing plant names to noticing patterns in community composition and asking broader ecological questions, such as the effects of disturbance on plant communities [K1/A2]. Taking students out of the classroom and into the field helped me show them the natural world as a place of inquiry, rather than just a place to learn content.

Beyond field instruction, I hold weekly office hours for the Applied Statistical Modeling for Environmental Management course, creating space for students to ask questions about coding challenges or lecture content in a focused, one-on-one format [A4]. But these office hours also serve another purpose: I intentionally ask students how the course is feeling for them and invite conversation about their personal and professional goals by sharing about mine as well [V1]. These are questions that I don’t have time to ask during lab sessions. Building these personal connections creates trust rapport that transforms office hours from a simple help session into genuine mentoring relationships, where students feel comfortable being vulnerable about what they do not understand.

Engaging in Professional Development as a Teacher

Through Duke’s Certificate in College Teaching, I have taken courses in Fundamentals of College Teaching and Teaching in Biology, which have fundamentally shaped how I approach course design and student engagement. In Teaching in Biology, for example, we read research on effective assessment methods and applied these strategies directly to our own lesson plans. This work helped me move from intuitive teaching decisions to intentionally structuring lessons with assessments grounded in how students are learning [V3].

Currently, I am participating in the Teaching on Purpose Fellowship through Duke’s Kenan Institute for Ethics, where we grapple with foundational questions like “Why teach?” and “Why learn?” These conversations push me to articulate not just how I teach content, but what purpose my teaching serves beyond delivering content. These reflections have deepened my commitment to helping students not only develop disciplinary knowledge, but critical and ethical reasoning that will follow them beyond my classroom [V5].
